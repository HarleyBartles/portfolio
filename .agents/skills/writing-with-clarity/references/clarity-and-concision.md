# Clarity and concision

Use this reference when prose is vague, padded, passive, abstract, indirect,
or more difficult than the underlying idea.

## Working rules

- Prefer an active construction when it makes the actor clearer and the sentence
  more direct. Keep the passive when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, or less
  important than the action's recipient.
- State the claim directly. Do not use hedging, negation, or ceremony to avoid
  saying what the evidence supports.
- Replace abstract labels with concrete people, actions, objects, dates,
  decisions, or consequences when the reader needs specifics.
- Remove words that add no meaning, repeat a nearby idea, or merely announce
  that the sentence is about to begin.
- Prefer familiar words unless a technical term is more precise for this
  audience. Define unavoidable jargon at first use.
- Preserve necessary uncertainty. Concision is not permission to turn a
  qualified claim into a certain one.
- Keep the most important word or consequence in a position the reader will
  notice, often near the end of the sentence or paragraph.

## Before and after

Weak: `A decision was made by the team to make a change to the backup process.`

Stronger: `The team changed the backup process.`

The stronger version is not automatically correct if the actor, process, or
degree of change is unknown. Revise toward the facts, not toward a formula.
