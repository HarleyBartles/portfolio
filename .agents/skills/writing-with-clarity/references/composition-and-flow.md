# Composition and flow

Use this reference when a document, paragraph, explanation, or report is hard
to follow even though its individual sentences are grammatical.

## Working rules

- Give each paragraph one dominant job. Split a paragraph when the reader must
  track a new topic, reason, phase, or conclusion.
- State the paragraph's point early when the reader needs orientation. The
  remaining sentences should explain, support, qualify, or develop that point.
- Order material according to the reader's task: conclusion before detail when
  speed matters; context before conclusion when the context changes its meaning.
- Keep related words and ideas close together. Do not separate a noun from its
  important qualifier or an action from its consequence.
- Use parallel forms for parallel ideas. If a list compares actions, keep the
  actions in the same grammatical shape.
- Keep summaries in one tense unless a deliberate time change is necessary.
- Vary sentence shape when repetition becomes mechanical, but do not vary it
  at the cost of clarity.
- End a paragraph or section with the consequence, decision, or memorable point
  when that helps the reader understand why the material matters.

## Review questions

1. Can each paragraph be summarized in one sentence?
2. Does each heading promise what the following text delivers?
3. Does the order match the reader's decision or task?
4. Are transitions explicit where the relationship is not obvious?
