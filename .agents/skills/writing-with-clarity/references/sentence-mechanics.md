# Sentence mechanics

Use this reference for sentence-level grammar and punctuation. Correct the
reader-facing problem without changing the author's intended meaning.

## Working rules

- Make ownership clear. Use possessives consistently, but prefer a rewrite when
  an apostrophe would make a technical name or product term confusing.
- Use commas to separate items in a series when the surrounding style calls for
  them. Keep parallel items visibly parallel.
- Set off parenthetical material only when it is genuinely supplementary. Do
  not insert one comma and forget its partner.
- Join independent clauses with a conjunction, semicolon, or full stop. Do not
  use a comma alone to carry two complete thoughts.
- Do not create fragments accidentally. A fragment may be deliberate in UI,
  headings, dialogue, or emphatic prose when the context makes that intent
  unmistakable.
- Attach an opening modifier to the grammatical subject that performs the
  action. Rewrite the sentence when the modifier can attach to the wrong noun.
- Prefer the simplest punctuation that preserves the relationship between
  ideas. Do not add punctuation merely to make prose look formal.

## Review questions

1. Can the reader tell who did what?
2. Are clauses joined with the mark or conjunction their relationship needs?
3. Does every opening phrase modify the intended subject?
4. Would a full stop improve a sentence carrying too many relationships?
