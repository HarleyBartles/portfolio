# Usage and word choice

Use this reference when a word, phrase, or term may confuse the reader or make
the prose sound inflated, imprecise, dated, or accidentally ambiguous.

## Working rules

- Choose the word that names the intended relationship, not merely a familiar
  synonym. Check whether a term means cause, correlation, sequence, contrast,
  or addition.
- Replace vague intensifiers with the measurable fact or concrete consequence
  they are trying to express.
- Remove clichés and stock phrases when they hide the actual point.
- Avoid jargon when a plain term is equally precise. Keep domain terminology
  when removing it would make the prose less accurate for its audience.
- Check words that are commonly confused, especially when a small distinction
  changes the claim.
- Prefer one stable term for one concept in the same document unless the
  variation is intentional and clear.
- Do not silently modernize a quotation, legal term, product name, or proper
  noun. Preserve it and explain it when necessary.

## Review questions

1. Could a reasonable reader interpret this word in two materially different
   ways?
2. Is the chosen term precise for this audience?
3. Does the wording claim more than the evidence supports?
4. Would a concrete example make the intended meaning safer?
